Stein Discrepancy

Let $\Pcl = \{p \ll \mathsf{Leb} : \nabla \log p(x) \in \Lcl^1(\R^d)\}$ where $p(x)$ is the density of $p \in \Pcl$.

A Stein Operator is any operator which acts on a function class $\Gcl$ and maps $\Gcl \ni g \mapsto \Acl_p g$ such that $\E_{p}[\Acl_p g] = 0$. This operator need not be unique, but it is typical to choose the following.

Definition (Stein Operator). For a function $f : \R^d \to \R$, the linear differential operator $\Acl_p f$ is given by $$ \begin{aligned} \Acl_pf = \frac{1}{p(x)} \nabla (p(x) f(x)) = \nabla \log p(x) f(x) + \nabla f(x) \end{aligned} $$ If $f:\R^d \to \R^d$ the operator is instead $$ \Acl_pf = \frac{1}{p(x)} D[p(x)f(x)] = \nabla \log p(x) f(x)^T + Df(x) $$ (i.e. the appropriate gradients are replaced by Jacobian). In downstream computations, this matrix-valued definition is often reduced to a scalar by taking the trace (cf. Stein Discrepancy below). Alternatively, we could take $$ A_p f = \Tr \Acl_p f = \langle g(x), \nabla \log p(x)\rangle + \nabla \cdot f $$ which is none other than the generator of Langevin diffusion! This formulation is called the Langevin stein operator.

Under sufficient regularity conditions on $f$, namely that it is differentiable and that it vanishes at the boundary of $\supp p$, one may integrate by parts to see that $$ \begin{aligned} \E_{p}[\Acl_p f(x)] = \int \nabla (p(x) f(x))\, dx = 0 \end{aligned} $$

Definition (Stein Class) The stein class of $p$ is the functions for which $$ \E_p[\Acl_p f(x)] = 0 $$

It turns out that the Stein operator (and associated class) uniquely determine $p$.

Theorem (Stein Discrepancy) for $p, q \in \Pcl$ we have $p = q$ if and only if $$ \sup_{f \in \Fcl} \E_q[\Tr \Acl_p f(x)]^2 = 0 $$ where $\Fcl$ is the Stein class of $p$. See Lee & Swan 2013, Stein's density approach and information inequalities.

One can also take $\Fcl$ to be a restricted class of functions, like Lipschitz or RKHS classes, leading to different Stein discrepancy metrics. (cf. Kernelized Stein Discrepancy).

Comparison to other metrics

It would be worth reading into Stein's Method for proving CLTs and understanding better the interplay between convergence of Markov Processes and inequalities between Stein Discrepancy and f-Divergences/Wasserstein metric. The relationships between LSI, dirichlet energy, and convergence of Markov Processes in different f-divergences, is a guiding example for what relationships to understand better about Stein's method for CLT.
