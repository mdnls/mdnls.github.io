Pseudospectral Approximation

Let $Q_m(f)$ be a quadrature scheme that exactly integrates polynomials up to degree $2m-1$ on the interval $[-1, 1]$ and let $(\Psi_\alpha(f))_{\alpha \geq 0}$ be an orthonormal polynomial basis of $\Lcl^2([-1, 1])$ WITH

Definition: let the pseudospectral approximation $S_{p, m}(f)$ of a function $f \in \Lcl^2(\R)$ is given by, $$ S_{p, m}(f) = \sum_{\alpha=0}^p \frac{Q_m(f \Phi_\alpha)}{\|\Psi_\alpha\|^2} \Psi_\alpha(x). $$

Remarks:

  • Intuition:
    • A reasonable spectral approximation of $f$ would be given by its projection onto the first $p$ basis functions, $(\Psi_\alpha)_{0 \leq \alpha < p}$.
    • In a pseudo-spectral approximation, we approximate the projection onto low-degree polynomials using quadrature. $$Q_m (f \Psi_\alpha) \approx \langle f, \Psi_\alpha\rangle_{\Lcl^2([-1, 1])}$$
  • Aliasing error: additional error due to discretization. In this case, the pseudospectral approximation has aliasing error in its basis coefficients.
  • Parameter choice: one might ask that $S_{p, m}(f)$ be "accurate on its range," i.e., that $S_{p, m}(f) = f$ whenever $f$ is a degree $p$ polynomial. To achieve zero aliasing error, we must have $2m-1 \geq \deg(f \Psi_\alpha)$ for all $\alpha \geq 0$. Taking the worst case on the right hand side, $2m-1 \geq 2p \implies m \geq p + 1/2$, so we take integer $m=p+1$ and $S_p(f) \coloneqq S_{p, p+1}(f)$.
  • Interpolation by quadrature: in a Gaussian quadrature scheme, the integral of $f$ is approximated by the integral of a polynomial interpolant of $(f(x_i))_{i=1}^m$ at the quadrature knots $(x_i)_{i=1}^m$. Because of this, the pseudospectral approximation interpolates $(f(x_i))_{i=1}^m$, because it is an exact approximation of the polynomial interpolation that is implicitly constructed by quadrature.
  • Extension to multiple dimensions: using tensorization/iterated integration, this approximation extends to higher dimensions.
