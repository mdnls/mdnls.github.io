Empirical Risk Minimization

Let $\Pcl = \{p_\theta : \theta \in \Theta\}$ be a parametric class of distributions and let $X_1 \ldots X_n \sim p^*$ be i.i.d. samples. Empirical Risk Minimization is an algorithm for choosing $\theta \in \Theta$ to estimate the distribution of the samples.

ERM is defined in terms of a loss function $l(x, \theta)$, which represents some notion of 'accuracy' or 'consistency' of the sample $x$ with respect to the parameter $\theta$. A common choice is negative log likelihood, $l(x, \theta) = -\log p_\theta(x)$. This choice leads leads to maximum likelihood estimation, where the parameter is selected which maximizes the likelihood of the observations.

The population risk is: $$ \Rcl(\theta) = \E_{X \sim p^*}[l(x; \theta)] $$ and the empirical risk is: $$ \hat{\Rcl}(\theta) = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n l(X_i, \theta). $$

The ERM estimator is then given by $\hat{\theta} = \argmin_{\theta \in \Theta} \hat{\Rcl}(\theta)$.

Examples

  • Ordinary Least-Squares: a simple generalization of empirical risk minimization can be used to estimate conditional relationships. Suppose we observe samples $\{(X_i, Y_i)\}_{i=1}^n \sim \pi$ and we would like to estimate a model $F_\theta(X_i) \approx Y_i$ where $\Fcl \ni F_\theta$ is a parametric model class. Intuitively, this model is approximating the conditional relationship $Y \mid X$, and the least-squares loss is given by

$$ l(x, y, \theta) = (y-F_\theta(x))^2. $$
