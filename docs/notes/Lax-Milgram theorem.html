Lax-Milgram Theorem

Theorem (Lax-Milgram). let $H$ be a Hilbert space and suppose that $B: H \times H \to \C$ is a bilinear form that is,

  1. Strongly positive definite: $B(u, u) \geq \mu \|u\|_H^2$ for some $\mu > 0$ and all $u \in H$.
  2. Strongly quadratically dominated: $B(u, v) \leq \theta \|u\|_H \|v\|_H$ for some $\theta \geq \mu$.

Then, for each $f \in H^*$, there exists a unique $u \in H$ such that $$ B(u, v) = \langle f, v\rangle \qquad \forall\ v \in H $$

For the sake of illustration, imagine the finite dimensional version of this statement. If $B$ is a strongly positive definite matrix, then it is invertible, and we can choose $u = B^{-T} f$ which realizes $u^T B v = f^T v$ for any $v$. In other words, the Lax-Milgram theorem provides some sufficient conditions for 'inverting' a generic bilinear form on a Hilbert space.

Proof. For any $u \in H$, the map $v \mapsto B(u, v)$ is represented by some element $w \in H$ such that $B[u, v] = \langle w, v\rangle_H$ for any $v \in H$. Let $A:H \to H$ be the mapping $w = Au$.

We must show that $\mathsf{Im}(A) = H$. First we will show that it is a linear, injective, bounded map, then we will argue that this forces surjectivity within $H$.

  1. Linearity: if $w_1 = A u_1$ and $w_2 = A u_2$ then $$ \langle \alpha w_1 + \beta w_2, v \rangle_H = \alpha B(u_1, v) + \beta B(u_2, v) = B(\alpha u_1 + \beta u_2, v)$$and we see that $A(\alpha u_1 + \beta u_2) = \alpha A u_1 + \beta A u_2$.
  2. Injectivity: if $A u_1 = A u_2$ then by linearity, $A (u_1 - u_2) = 0$. But this is impossible because

$$ \mu\|Au\|^2_H \leq B(Au, A u) = \langle u, A u \rangle_H \leq \|u\|_H \|A u\|_H \quad \implies \quad \mu \|Au\|_H \leq \|u\|$$

  1. Boundedness: we have $$\|A u\|_H^2 = B (u, Au) \leq \theta \|u\|_H \|Au\|_H \quad \implies \quad \|Au\|_H \leq \theta \|u\|_H$$

Properties (2) and (3) imply that $\mathsf{Im}(A)$ is closed: if $(v_k)_{k \geq 0}$ converges to $\mathsf{Im}(A)$, then there exists $u_k \in \mathsf{Im}(A)$ so $\|A v_k - u_k\|_H \to 0$, and boundedness of $A$ allows to conclude that $v_k, u_k$ converges to points $v_\infty, u_\infty$ with $A v_\infty = u_\infty$.

Since $\mathsf{Im}(A)$ is a closed subspace of $H$, we can decompose $H = \mathsf{Im}(A) \oplus \mathsf{Im}(A)^{\perp}$, but injectivity forces that $\mathsf{Im}(A)^{\perp} = \{0\}$.

Finally, by the representer theorem, there exists $w^* \in H$ such that $\langle f, v \rangle = \langle w^*, v \rangle_H$ for all $v \in H$, and we may choose $u$ the solution of $A u = w^*$.
