Representation of martingales as stochastic integrals

It is a common folklore statement that any continuous martingale can be thought of as a stochastic integral with respect to Brownian motion. That is, if $M$ is a martingale, then it can be written $$ M_t = C + \int_0^t h_s \, d B_s \qquad C \in \R $$ for some process $h$. This is in fact true in a surprising amount of generality!

Proposition (martingales as stochastic integrals). Let $B$ be a real Brownian motion on a probability space $(\Omega, \Fcl, P)$ and assume that $(\Fcl_t)_{t \geq 0} \subseteq \Fcl$ is the completed canonical filtration of $B$. Then, if $M$ is a martingale bounded in $\Lcl^2$, there exists a unique progressive process $h \in \Lcl^2(B)$ (ie. $\E[\int_0^\infty h_s^2\, ds] < \infty$) such that $$ M_t = C + \int_0^t h_s \, dB_s. $$ If instead $M$ is a continuous local martingale, the same holds with $h \in \Lcl^2_{\text{loc}}(B)$.

Proof. First, note that it is sufficient to prove the statement for $M_\infty$ (ie. only at the point $t = \infty$) since we can recover $$ M_t = \E[M_\infty \mid \Fcl_t] = C + \E\left[ \int_0^\infty h_s \, dB_s \mid \Fcl_t\right] = C + \int_0^t h_s \, d B_s. $$ As usual, if instead $M$ is a continuous local martingale, then we can replace it with $M^a$ for $a \in \R$ and take the limit $a \to \infty$.

One martingale that is easy to write as a stochastic integral is the exponential martingale of Brownian motion, which has $\Ecl(\lambda B)_t = 1 + \lambda \int_0^t \Ecl(\lambda B)_s \, d B_s$. More generally, for any $f(s) = \sum_{i=1}^p \lambda_i 1_{(t_{i-1}, t_i]}(s)$, the process $X_t = i \int_0^t f(s) \, dB_s$ satisfies $$ \Ecl(X)_\infty = \exp \left( i \sum_{i=1}^p \lambda_i (B_{t_i} - B_{t_{i-1}}) + \frac{1}{2} \sum_{i=1}^p \lambda_i^2 (t_i - t_{i-1}) \right) = 1 + i \int_0^\infty \Ecl(X)_s \, f(s) \, d B_s $$

We will argue that the linear span of such processes (call it $\Scl$) makes up a dense subspace of $\Lcl^2_\C(\Omega, \Fcl_\infty, P)$. It will follow that every $Z \in \Lcl^2_\C(\Omega, \Fcl_\infty, P)$ admits a limit $Z^{(n)} \stackrel{\Lcl^2}{\longrightarrow} Z$ of the form, $$ Z^{(n)} = \E[Z^{(n)}] + \int_0^\infty h_s^{(n)} \, d B_s. $$ Moreover, since $\E[(Z^{(n)})^2] = (\E[Z^{(n)}])^2 + \int_0^\infty (h^{(n)}_s)^2 \, d B_s$, the corresponding $h^{(n)}_s$ are Cauchy in $\Lcl^2(B)$ and so they have a limit $h_s \in \Lcl^2(B)$.

Suppose that $Z$ is orthogonal to $\Scl$. Then, $$ \E\left[ Z \exp \left( i \sum_{i=1}^p \lambda_i(B_{t_i}-B_{t_{i-1}})\right)\right] = 0 $$ for any choice of $(\lambda_i)_{i=1}^p$ and $(t_i)_{i=0}^p$. Taking the (inverse) Fourier transform implies that the measure $\mu$ defined on $\sigma(B_{t_0}, B_{t_1} - B_{t_0}, \ldots, B_{t_p}-B_{t_{p-1}})$ by the identity $$ \mu(F) \coloneqq \E\left[ Z 1_F(B_{t_0}, B_{t_1} - B_{t_0}, \ldots, B_{t_p} - B_{t_{p-1}})\right] $$ is identically zero, which forces $Z = 0$ and thus $\Scl = \Lcl^2_\C(\Omega, \Fcl_\infty, P)$.

It remains to show that the integral representation is unique. Suppose that $$ Z = C + \int_0^\infty h_s \, d B_s = C + \int_0^\infty h_s' \, dB_s $$ then it follows $$ \E \left[ \int _0^\infty (h_s - h'_s)^2 \, d B_s \right] = \E\left[ \left( \int_0^\infty h_s \, d B_s - \int_0^\infty h'_s \, d B_s\right)^2\right] = 0 $$ so $h, h'$ are equal.
