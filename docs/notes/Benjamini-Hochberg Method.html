Benjamini-Hochberg Method

Consider a multiple testing problem with nulls $H_{0, i}$ and alternatives $H_{1, i}$ for $i = 1 \ldots n$.

Benjamini-Hochberg Procedure. for a specific realization of p-values $\{p_1, \ldots, p_n\}$, let $p_{(i)}$ denote the $i$-th smallest value and let $$ i_0 = \argmax\ \{i \in [n] : p_{(i)} \leq \alpha i / n\} $$ The Benjamini-Hochberg test rejects $R := \{H_{(i)} : 1 \leq i \leq i_0\}$.

This test is designed to control the False Discovery Rate at level $\alpha$.

Theorem (FDR control): suppose the p-values are independent and super-uniform. Then, for any $L \subseteq [n]$ such that $H_{0, i}$ holds for $i \in L$ and $H_{1, i}$ holds for $i \not \in L$, we have $$ \E_{H_L}\left[ \frac{|L \cap R|}{|R| \lor 1} \right] \leq \alpha $$

Proof. the idea is to study the random variables $|R| \lor 1$ and $V_i := 1\{H_i \in R\}$ and to get a bound by decoupling them. To that end, consider the $\sigma$-algebra $\Fcl_{-i} = \sigma(p_1, \ldots, p_{i-1}, p_{i+1}, p_n)$ and the random variable $R_i = R \cup \{H_i\}$. Since $R_i$ contains $H_i$ always, it is measurable with respect to $\Fcl_{-i}$.

We have $$ |L \cap R| = \sum_{i \in L} V_i \implies \E_{H_L}\left[ \frac{| L \cap R|}{|L|}\right] = \sum_{i \in L} \frac{V_i}{|R|\lor 1} $$ Each term in the sum is given by $$ \begin{aligned} \frac{V_i}{|R|\lor 1} & = \sum_{k=1}^n \frac{V_i 1\{|R| = k\}}{k} = \sum_{i=1}^n \frac{V_i 1\{|R_i| = k\}}{k} \end{aligned} $$ since $V_i = 1$ precisely when $H_i \in R$ and thus when $|R_i| = |R|$. Taking the expectation on both sides, $$ \E\left[ \frac{V_i}{|R|\lor 1}\right] = \sum_{k=1}^n \frac{\E[\E[V_i 1\{|R_i| = k\} \mid \Fcl_{-i}]]}{k} = \sum_{k=1}^n \frac{\E[1\{|R_i|=k\}]}{k} P_{H_L}[p_i \leq \alpha k /n] \leq \frac{\alpha}{n} $$ where the last inequality follows from the fact that under $H_L$, $p_i$ is super uniform because we assumed $i \in L$.

Further results:

  • For dependent and marginally uniform p-values, Benjamini and Yekuteli (2001) show that B-H has FDR less than $\alpha S(n)$ where $S(n) \coloneqq \log n + \gamma$ and $\gamma$ is the Euler-Mascheroni constant.
