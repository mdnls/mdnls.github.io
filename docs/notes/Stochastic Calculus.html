Continuous-time Martingales

  • Continuous Stochastic Processes
    • Filtration
    • Stopping Time
    • Optional Stopping Theorem
  • Continuous-time Martingales
    • Structural properties
      • Regularity: one can show in surprising generality that supermartingales admit cádlág modifications. Specifically, by analyzing Upcrossing Numbers, one can argue that a supermartingale $X_t$ has values which cannot fluctuate in a way that prevents the existence of a limit $X_{t+} = \lim_{\Q \ \ni \ s \to t+} X_{s}$. Then $Y_t \coloneqq X_{t+}$ is a cádlág modification of $X_t$.
      • Convergence: under appropriate boundedness assumptions, there exists $X_\infty \in \Lcl^1$ to which $X_t$ converges as $t \to \infty$. This convergence is almost sure when $X_t$ is $\Lcl^1$-bounded and it is in $\Lcl^1$ when $X_t$ is u.i.

The proof of this is a good example of how to apply Itô's formula to a function that is only twice differentiable on a subset $U \subseteq \R^n$ that contains $M_t$ almost surely.

Stochastic Integration

  • Stochastic Integration
    • Construction of the Stochastic Integral
  • Interesting results that can be proven with stochastic integration
    • Intuition: quadratic variation characterizes a martingale's sample paths.
      • Levy's characterization of Brownian Motion: a continuous adapted process $X$ with $\langle X \rangle_t = t$ a.s. must be a Brownian motion.
      • Dambis-Dubin-Schwartz: if $M$ is a continuous local martingale with $\langle M \rangle _\infty = \infty$ a.s., then there exists a brownian motion $(\beta_t)_{t \geq 0}$ for which $M_t = \beta_{\langle M \rangle_t}$ a.s. for all $t \geq 0$. The intuition is that since $\langle \beta \rangle_t = t$, the effect of time rescaling is that $\langle \beta_{\langle M \rangle_\cdot} \rangle_t = \langle M \rangle_t$ and, in turn, the processes are a.s. equal.
    • Burkholder-Davis-Gundy Inequalities: for every stopping time $T$, and every $p > 0$, $\| \sup_{0 \leq s \leq T} M_s \|_{\Lcl^p(P)} \cong \| \sqrt{\langle M \rangle_T }\|_{\Lcl^p(P)}$ up to constants that depend only on $p > 0$. This is especially useful when $M$ is a stochastic integral, since the bracket commutes!
    • Representation of martingales as stochastic integrals

    • Schilder's Theorem looks very cool!

Review Questions

  1. Gaussian Spaces
    1. Prove that: let $X, Y$ be jointly multivariate Gaussian. Then they are independent iff they are uncorrelated. (Hint: use their characteristic functions)
    2. In the above setting, compute the law of $X \mid Y$. (Hint: perform Graham-Schmidt)
  2. Constructing Brownian motion.
    1. Define Gaussian white noise and describe a construction between spaces of your choice.
    2. Explain the difference between a stochastic process and its sample paths. What does it mean for one process to be a 'modification' of another? What does it mean for two processes to be 'indistinguishable'?
    3. Prove that: let $\Hcl = (\Omega, \Ecl, P)$ be a Gaussian space with measure $P$. Let $G$ be a Gaussian white noise on $\Lcl^2(\R_+, \R, \mathsf{Leb})$ mapping into $\Hcl$. Then $G$ admits a modification with continuous sample paths. Hints follow:
      1. First, prove the following. suppose there exists a countable dense subset of $\R_+$ such that for a.e. $\omega \in \Omega$, $s \mapsto X_s(\omega)$ is locally Holder continuous. Then $X_s$ admits a continuous modification that is unique up to indistinguishability.
      2. Next, use a chaining argument to prove that this is indeed the case for brownian motion on the dyadic set $D = \{i 2^{-k} : k, i \in \Z\}$.
    4. Define the Wiener measure.
  3. Continuous stochastic processes
    1. Stopping times
      1. Define a stopping time. One definition is that $\{T \leq t\} \in \Fcl_t$ for $t > 0$. Is it equivalent to define a stopping time as a random variable with $\{T < t\} \in \Fcl_t$? Why or why not.
      2. Let $O, F \subseteq \R^d$ be open and closed respectively. Let $T_O = \inf\{t \geq 0 : X_t \in O\}$ and define $T_F$ similarly. Prove that these random variables are stopping times (hint: you will have to identify which filtrations they are stopping times with respect to)
    2. Filtrations
      1. Define a filtration
      2. What does it mean for a process to be adapted? To be progressive? Can you state and prove any conditions where one of these properties imply the other?
    3. Martingale representation theorems
      1. Give a definition of a closed martingale. Can you state and prove any conditions under which a martingale is guaranteed to be closed?
    4. Optional stopping
      1. Legall pp 62-63, prove some of these examples yourself.
