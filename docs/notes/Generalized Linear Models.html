Generalized Linear Models

Definition: the Generalized Linear Model can be thought of as an extension of Exponential Families to the case of conditional data.

Suppose $(x_i, y_i) \sim \pi$ with $x_i \in \R^d$ and $y_i \in \R$. A GLM is an exponential family over $y\mid x$ with sufficient statistic $\phi(y) = y$ and parameter $\langle x, \beta^* \rangle$ for some $\beta^* \in \R^d$: $$ p_{\beta^*}(y \mid x) = h(y) \exp(\langle x, \beta^* \rangle y - \Phi(\langle x, \beta^* \rangle ) ) $$

Relationship to Linear Regression: the GLM can be used as a regression model for $\E[y \mid x]$. The exponential family satisfies $\E[y \mid x] = (\nabla \Phi)(\langle x, \beta^* \rangle)$

GLMs are often described as "linear regression plus a nonlinearity," in which case $\nabla \Phi$ is the nonlinear part. When $(\nabla \Phi)(x) = x$ the model recovers traditional linear regression.

Examples:

  • Poisson Regression: for $\Ycl = \N$, the Poisson density with parameter $\lambda > 0$ is $\frac{\lambda^y e^{-\lambda}}{y!}$. Mapping $\lambda = e^{\langle x, \beta^* \rangle }$ leads to, $$

p_{\beta^*}(y \mid x) = \frac{1}{y!} \exp \left( \langle x, \beta^* \rangle y - \Phi(\langle x, \beta^* \rangle)\right) \qquad \Phi(s) = e^s $$and the corresponding maximum likelihood estimator is $$ \hat{\beta} \in \arg \min_{\beta \in \R^d} \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n \left(\langle x_i, \beta \rangle y_i - e^{\langle x_i, \beta\rangle} \right) $$
