Finite-variation Process

Definition (finite-variation process). an adapted process $(X_t)_{t \geq 0}$ is finite variation if, for each $\omega \in \Omega$, the sample path $t \mapsto X_t(\omega)$ is a Finite-variation Function.

Any finite variation process can be decomposed into the difference of two increasing processes. Specifically, setting $$ V_t = \int_0^t |d X_s| $$ yields a process that is increasing by construction, and for which $$ X_t = \frac{1}{2} \underbrace{\left( V_t + X_t\right)}_{X_t^+} - \frac{1}{2}\underbrace{\left(V_t - X_t \right)}_{X_t^-}. $$

Integration for Finite-variation Processes

It is easy to define a notion of integration for finite-variation processes based on the corresponding notion of integration with respect to a Finite-variation Function. Specifically, for any progressive process $(F_t)_{t \geq 0}$ and any adapted and finite variation $(X_t)_{t \geq 0}$, the quantity $$ (H \cdot X)_t \coloneqq \int_0^t H_s \, d X_s $$ is well defined (pointwise for each $\omega \in \Omega$) as the integral of the measurable function $t \mapsto H_t(\omega)$ with respect to the finite variation function $t \mapsto X_t(\omega)$.

Proposition (finite variation integrals of finite variation processes) suppose that for all $t \geq 0$ and $\omega \in \Omega$, $$ \int_0^t |H_s(\omega)| \, |d X_s(\omega)| < \infty. $$ Then $(H \cdot X)$ is in fact also a finite variation process.

Proof. see integrating finite-variation functions.

Remark. It may be the case that the finiteness condition only holds for almost every $\omega \in \Omega$. In this case, if $(\Fcl_t)_{t \geq 0}$ is complete, then we can define $H'_s(\omega) = H_s(\omega) 1_{\omega \not \in N}$ where $N$ is the null set of $\omega$ where the condition does not hold, and this process is still progressive. We can define $(H \cdot X) = (H' \cdot X)$ and proceed.
