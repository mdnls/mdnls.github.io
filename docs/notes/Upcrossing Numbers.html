Upcrossing Numbers

In the theory of continuous time martingales, upcrossing numbers can be used to prove regularity results in fairly general settings. For example, they are crucial in the proof that supermartingales admit c치dl치g modifications. Here are two key tools:

Definition (upcrossing number). Let $f: \R \to \R$ be a function and let $a < b$ be real numbers. For any interval $I \subseteq \R$, the upcrossing number $M_{ab}^f(I)$ is the smallest $k \geq 1$ for which there exists a sequence of pairs $\{(t_i, s_i)\}_{i \leq k} \subseteq I$ for which, $$ t_1 < s_1 < t_2 < s_2 < \ldots < t_k < s_k $$ and such that $f(t_i) \leq a$ while $f(s_i) \geq b$ for all $i = 1, \ldots, k$.

Generally speaking, upcrossing numbers are used to prove that a process defined on $D$ admits a right (or left) continuous extension to $\R$.

Proposition (upcrossing numbers imply limits exist). let $D$ be a countable dense subset of $\R_+$ and let $f$ be a real-valued function on $D$. Suppose that for every $T \in D$,

  1. $f$ is bounded on $D \cap [0, T]$.
  2. for every $a < b$ the upcrossing number $M^f_{ab}(D \cap [0, T])< \infty$.

Then, the right limit $f(t+) \coloneqq \lim_{D \ \ni \ s \to t +} f(s)$ exists for each $t \geq 0$, and similarly the left limit $f(t-) = \lim_{D \ \ni \ s \to t-} f(s)$ exists for all $t > 0$. As a consequence, the function $g(s) \coloneqq f(t+)$ is a c치dl치g function that coincides with $f$ on $D$.

In light of this lemma, proving that a random process admits a continuous modification is essentially reduced to checking that its upcrossing numbers are finite for any $[a, b] \subseteq I$. The standard tool to do this is Doob's upcrossing inequality.

Proposition (Doob's upcrossing inequality). suppose that $(X_t)_{t \geq 0}$ is a supermartingale and let $D$ be any dense, countable subset of $\R$. Then, for any $a < b$, $$ \E[M^X_{ab}(D \cap [0, T])] \leq \frac{1}{b-a} \E[(X_T-a)^-] $$ and in particular the upcrossing numbers are finite when $(X_t)_{t \geq 0}$ is $\Lcl^1$ bounded.

Note that $x^- = -\min(x, 0)$.
