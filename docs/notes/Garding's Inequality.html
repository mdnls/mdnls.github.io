Garding's Inequality

Theorem (Garding Inequality for 2nd order PDE). Let $L$ be a second order, divergence form, linear differential operator: $$ Lf(x) = -\sum_{i, j = 1}^n \partial_{x_i}(\alpha_{ij}(x) \partial_{x_j} f(x)) + \sum_{i=1}^n b_i(x) \partial_{x_i} f(x) + c(x) f(x). $$ Call $A(x)$ the matrix $A(x)_{ij} = a_{ij}(x)$. We assume that:

  1. Uniform ellipticity: $A(x) \succcurlyeq \mu I$ for all $x$.
  2. Boundedness: $a_{ij}, b_i, c \in \Lcl^\infty(\Omega)$ for all $i, j = 1 \ldots n$.

This operator has the associated bilinear form: $$ B(u, v) = \sum{i, j = 1}^n \int{\Omega} A{ij} \partial{xi} u \partial{xj} v + bi \partial{xi}u v + c u v \, dx $$where dependence on $x$ is suppressed inside the integrand.

The bilinear form satisfies the inequality, $$ B(u, u) + C \|u\|_{\Lcl^2(\Omega)}^2 \geq c\|u\|_{H^1(\Omega)} $$ for a constants $C \gg c > 0$ that depends only on $\Omega$ and $L$.

Garding's inequality holds more generally for $k$-th order uniformly elliptic PDEs, in which case the condition takes the form $$ A(u, u, \ldots, u) \geq \mu \|k\|^{2k} $$ where $A : \Omega \to (\R^n)^{\otimes k}$ is the tensor of coefficients of $k$-th order terms in $L$. In that case, the inequality states $$ B(u, u) + C \|u\|^2_{\Lcl^2(\Omega)} \geq \|u\|_{H^k_0(\Omega)}. $$

Proof of the second order case. it is immediate that, $$ \langle \nabla u, A \nabla u \rangle_{\Lcl^2(\Omega)} \geq \mu \|\nabla u\|_{\Lcl^2(\Omega)} $$ where $C$ is above the PoincarÃ© constant of $\Omega$. If $C$ is also above $\|b_i\|_{\Lcl^\infty(\Omega)}, \|c\|_{\Lcl^\infty(\Omega)}$ then $$ \langle c u, u\rangle_{\Lcl^2(\Omega)} \leq C \|u\|_{\Lcl^2(\Omega)} $$ and also, by Fenchel-Young, $$ \langle b \partial_{x_i} u, u\rangle_{\Lcl^2(\Omega)} \leq \frac{C}{2 \eps} \|\nabla u\|_{\Lcl^2} + \frac{\eps}{2} \|u\|_{\Lcl^2(\Omega)}. $$ For sufficiently large $\eps > 0$, the first term is small enough that $$ B(u, u) \geq (C^{-1}\mu - \eps ') \|\nabla u\|_{\Lcl^2(\Omega)} - O(\|u\|_{\Lcl^2(\Omega)}) $$ and the claim follows by adding $O(\|u\|_{\Lcl^2(\Omega)})$ to both sides.

This bound is extremely crude... But it illustrates an important concept. Uniform ellipticity lets us argue that the bilinear form is positive definite with respect to the highest derivatives that we expect u to have. The impact of lower derivatives is handled in a convenient, 'crude' way.

Application: existence of solutions to linear second order ODE

Proposition. for a second order PDE given in the form above, there exists $\gamma \geq 0$ such that for each $\lambda \geq \gamma$ and each $f \in \Lcl^2(\Omega)$, there exists a unique weak solution $u \in H^1_0(\Omega)$ of the problem $$ \begin{cases} Lu+\lambda u = f & x \in \Omega\\ u = 0 & x \in \partial \Omega \end{cases} $$

Proof. Observe that, taking $\mu$ to be larger than the constant $C$ of the Garding inequality, the operator $L_\lambda \coloneqq L + \lambda \mathsf{Id}$ has $B_\lambda(u, v) = B(u, v) + \lambda \langle u, v \rangle_H$ and in particular $B_\lambda(u, u) \gtrsim \|u\|_{H^1_0(\Omega)}$. Clearly (by boundedness of the coefficients) $B_\lambda$ is also quadratically dominated, so by the Lax-Milgram theorem there is a unique $u \in H^1_0(\Omega)$ so that $B_\lambda(u, v) = \langle u, f \rangle$.

Note that in the above theorem, we can actually take $f$ to belong to a larger class of functions than $\Lcl^2(\Omega)$. For the Lax-Milgram theorem we require that $f \in (H^1_0(\Omega))^*$, that is, the space $H^{-1}(\Omega)$.
