Global Sensitivity Analysis

Consider a random variable of the form $Y = f(X_1, X_2, \ldots, X_d)$. The goal of Global Sensitivity Analysis is to 'attribute' $\Var(Y)$ to randomness in each of the $(X_i)_{i \in [d]}$. By the Tower Law of Variance, $$ \Var(Y) = \underbrace{\Var_{x_i} (\E_{x_{-i}}[Y \mid X_i = x_i])}_{(1)} \ + \ \underbrace{\E_{x_i}[\Var_{x_{-i}}(Y \mid X_i = x_i)]}_{(2)} $$ The term $(1)$ is the variance explained by $x_i$ and the term $(2)$ is the unexplained variance.

Univariate Sensitivity Indices: based on the above formula, we may define the quantities,

  • Main Effect Sensitivity: the variance explained by $X_i$ normalized by $\Var(Y)$. $$

S_i = \frac{\Var(\E[Y \mid X_i]) }{\Var(Y)}$$

  • Total Effect Sensitivity: the complement of the variance explained by all variables except for $X_i$. $$

S_{T_i} = 1 - \frac{\Var(\E[Y \mid X_{-i}])}{\Var(Y)} = \frac{\E[\Var(Y \mid X_{-i})]}{\Var{(Y)}} $$ Multivariate Sensitivity Indices: suppose the $(X_i)_{i \in [d]}$ are mutually independent and that $f$ can be written $$ \begin{aligned} f(x_1, \ldots, x_d)& = f_0 + \sum_{i=1}^d f_i(x_i) \\ & \quad + \sum_{i=1}^d \sum_{j > i}^df_{ij}(x_i, x_j) \\ & \quad + \sum_{i=1}^d \sum_{j > i}^d \sum_{k > i}^d f_{ijk}(x_i, x_j, x_k) \\ & \quad + \ \ldots \\ & = \sum_{I\in 2^{[d]}}f_I ( (x_i)_{i \in I}). \end{aligned} $$ Also, suppose that the $(f_I)_{I \in 2^{[d]}}$ satisfy the following identifiability condition: for all nonempty $I \subseteq [d]$, for each $i \in I$, $$ \E_{x_i} [f_I((x_j)_{j \in I} ) \mid (x_j)_{j \not = i} ] = \int f_I((x_j)_{j \in I})\ p_i(x_i) \, d{x_i} = 0 $$

This representation is called a High-dimensional Model Representation (HDMR) and, under these assumptions, it satisfies:

  1. Independence: the functions $f_{I}$ are mean zero and uncorrelated.
  2. Hierarchical approximation:

Remarks:

  • Multivariate decomposition of variance: by the independence property, we can write

$$ \begin{aligned} \Var(Y) & = \Var\left( f_0 + \sum_{i=1}^d f_i(X_i) + \sum_{j > i}^d f_{ij} (X_i, X_j) + \ldots \right) \\ & = \sum_{i=1}^d \Var(f_i) + \sum_{j > i}^d \Var(f_{ij}) + \ldots \end{aligned}$$which leads to a decomposition of the variance into contributions from interaction terms of every possible order. This decomposition is known as the ANOVA Decomposition. Normalizing by the total variance leads to a multivariate version of the tower law: $$ 1 = \sum_{i=1}^d \underbrace{\frac{\Var(f_i)}{\Var(Y)}}_{S_i}+ \sum_{j > i}^d \underbrace{ \frac{\Var(f_{ij})}{\Var(Y)}}_{S_{ij}} + \ldots $$ with contributions from each of the terms $(S_I)_{I \subseteq [d]}$.

  • HDMR captures the complexity of interactions between $x_1, \ldots, x_d$. The hierarchical approximation property can be interpreted as separating out the high order correlations of $f$. If $f$ is low
  • Main effect of $x_i$ is $S_i$ and total effect of $x_i$ is $\sum_{I \subseteq [d], i \in I} S_{I}$.
