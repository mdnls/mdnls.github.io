Complete, Singular, and General Integral of a PDE

Definition (Complete Integral): consider the PDE $$ F(\nabla u, u, x) = 0 $$ where $F(p, z, x) : \R^n \times \R \times \R^n \to \R$ is given. A complete integral of the PDE is parametrized family of solutions $(u(x;a))_{a \in A}$ for $A \subseteq \R^n$ such that,

  1. For each $a \in A$, $x \mapsto u(x, a) \in C^2$.
  2. The matrix $M \coloneqq [\nabla_a u \ ; \ D_x \nabla_a u ]$ satisfies the rank condition $\text{rank}(M) = n$ for all $(x, a) \in \Omega \times A$.

The intuition behind the rank condition is that it characterizes the idea that $u$ depends on all $n$ parameters of $a \in A$. To see this, observe that if $u(x, a) = v(x, \psi(a))$ for some $v : \Omega \times B \to \R$, $B \subseteq \R^{n-1}$, and $\psi : A \to B$, then by the chain rule we have $$ \nabla_a u = \nabla_b v(x,\psi(a))^T[D \psi(a)] \qquad D_x \nabla_a u = [D_x \nabla_b v]^T [D \psi(a)] $$and so the rank of $M$ is bottlenecked by the rank of $D \psi(a) \in \R^{(n-1) \times n}$. In other words, if $\text{rank}(M) = n$, then there cannot exist $\psi : A \to \R^{n-1}$ such that $u(x, a) = v(x, \psi(a))$.

From a complete integral, we can construct additional solutions of a PDE.

Definition (Singular Integral a.k.a. envelope): for open sets $\Omega \subset \R^n$, $A \subset \R^m$, suppose $u(x, a) : \Omega \times A \to \R$ is $C^1$ function, and that the equation $$ D_a u(x, a) = 0 \qquad \text{for each }x \in \Omega $$ may be solved for $a$ as a continuous function $a = \varphi(x)$. Then $v(x) = u(x, \varphi(x))$ is the envelope of $u$. Observe that (by the chain rule) if $u$ solves the PDE $F(\nabla u, u, x)$ then so too does $v$.

Now, finally, we can further expand the range of solutions constructed from a complete integral using the general integral construction. I find this kind of unintuitive, but whatever.

Definition (General Integral) suppose we can construct $h : A' \to \R$ where $A \subseteq \R^n$ and $A' \subseteq \R^{n-1}$, such that the graph of $h$ is contained in $A$. Write $a = (a', h(a'))$ for $a' \in A'$ and $u'(x, a') = u(x, (a', h(a')))$ and finally $v$ the envelope of $u'$. Then $v$ is called the general integral of $u$.

The idea is that, in exchange for one degree of freedom, we can expand the parametrization of $u(x, a)$ so that it depends on $a' \in A'$ and a (potentially nonlinear) function $h : A' \to \R$.
