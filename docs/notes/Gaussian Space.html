Gaussian Space

Gaussian spaces are $\Lcl^2$ spaces that contain linear combinations of Gaussian random variables. In an $\Lcl^2$ space, the inner product of two random variables is simply their correlation. Because uncorrelated Gaussian RVs are independent, in Gaussian spaces, orthogonality is equivalent to independence and conditional expectation is captured by linear projection.

Definition. a Gaussian space is a closed linear subspace of a generic probability space $\Lcl^2(\Omega, \Bcl, P)$ that contains only centered Gaussian random variables.

Examples.

  1. Gaussian process space. Let $(X_t)_{t \in T}$ be a Gaussian process. Then the closed linear subspace given by the span of $(X_t)_{t \in T}$ is a Gaussian space. One must verify that the span is indeed closed. It follows from the fact that convergence in L2 is equivalent to convergence in distribution for Gaussians.
  2. Karhunen-Loeve Expansion. The K-L expansion can be viewed as furnishing a basis for a given Gaussian process space. Specifically, given a centered Gaussian process with covariance kernel $K(s, t)$, the eigenfunctions $\{\phi_k(t)\}_{k \geq 0}$ can be viewed as basis coefficients such that $X_t = \sum_{k \geq 0} Z_k \phi_k(t)$ where the $Z_k$ are orthonormal (ie. independent unit Gaussians).

Proposition: Gaussian Conditioning. let $X, Y \in \Hcl$ be elements in a Gaussian space $\Hcl$. Let $\sigma_{xy} = \E[XY]$, $\sigma_x^2 = \E[X^2]$, and $\sigma^2_y = \E[Y^2]$. Then $X$ can be decomposed $$ X = X^\perp + X^{||} $$ where $$ X^\perp = X - \frac{\langle X, Y\rangle_{\Hcl}}{\|Y\|_{\Hcl}^2} Y = X - \sigma_{xy}\sigma_y^{-2}Y $$ is the orthogonal complement, and $X^{||} = \sigma_{xy}\sigma_y^{-2} Y$ is the parallel part. Since orthogonality is equivalent to independence in Gaussian space, $X^\perp, Y$ are independent, so $$ \begin{aligned} \E[X^\perp \mid Y=y] & = \E[X^\perp]\\ & = 0 \\ \E[X \mid Y=y] & = \E[X^{||}\mid Y = y] \\ & = \sigma_{xy}\sigma_y^{-2} y\\ \end{aligned} $$ Moreover, it is easy to check that $\Cov[X^\perp] = \sigma_x^2 - \sigma_{xy}^2\sigma_y^{-1}$, so that $$ \mathsf{Law}(X \mid Y = y ) = \Ncl(\sigma_{xy} \sigma_y^{-2}y,\ \sigma_x^2 - \sigma_{xy}^2\sigma_y^{-1})$$

This idea can be generalized to multiple dimensions!

  • Let $X, Y$ be multivariate Gaussians with means $\mu_X, \mu_Y$ and covariances $\Cov[X] = \Sigma_X$, $\Cov[Y] = \Sigma_Y$, $\Cov[X, Y] = \Sigma_{XY}$. Then $X \mid Y$ is Gaussian with $$

\begin{align*} \E[X \mid Y = y] & = \mux + \Sigma{XY}\Sigma{Y}^{-1} (y - \muY) \\ \Cov[X \mid Y = y] & = \SigmaX - \Sigma{XY} \SigmaY^{-1} \Sigma{XY}. \end{align*}
