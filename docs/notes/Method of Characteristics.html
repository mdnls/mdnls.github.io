Method of Characteristics

The general idea of the method of characteristics is to solve a PDE by reducing it to an ODE along a parametrized curve in its domain. For example, consider the equation $$ (Lu)(t, x) = 0 $$ where $L : C^k(\R^d) \to C(\R^d)$ is some $k$-th order differential operator. In the method of characteristics, the goal is to construct a parametrized curve $X(t) : \R \to \R^d$ such that $(Lu)(t, X(t)) = 0$ is converted into a simple ODE in time. For example, see solving the transport PDE via Method of Characteristics.

General Method

Consider the nonlinear, first-order PDE $$ F(\nabla u, u, x) = 0 $$ where $F(p, z, x) : \R^n \times \R \times \R^n \to \R$ is a generic function. How can we derive the characteristic PDE associated with $F$? Introduce a parametrized curve $x(s) : I \to \R^n$ where $I \subseteq \R$ is an interval. Similarly, write $z(s) = u(x(s))$ and $p(s) = [\nabla u](x(s))$. What are the dynamics of $(x, z, p)$? Differentiating with respect to $s$ yields, $$ \begin{aligned} \dot{p}_i(s) & = \sum_{j=1}^n u_{x_i, x_j} \dot{x}_j(s) = [\nabla^2 u] \dot{x}(s) \\ \dot{z}(s) & = \langle \nabla u, \dot{x}(s)\rangle = \langle p, \dot{x}(s) \rangle \end{aligned} $$ But it is not clear how to compute $\dot{x}(s)$ or $D^2 u$. It turns out that we can use the PDE to compute a prescription for $\dot{x}(s)$ which eliminates the Hessian term, thereby closing the equations. To do so, differentiate the PDE with respect to $x$: $$ \begin{aligned} 0 = \nabla_x F(Du, u, x) & = [\nabla^2 u][\nabla_p F] + F_{z} \nabla u + \nabla_x F \quad \implies \quad [\nabla^2 u] [\nabla_p F] = - F_z \nabla u + \nabla_x F. \end{aligned} $$ So, if we set $\dot{x}(s) = \nabla_p F(p(s), z(s), x(s))$ then we arrive at the closed system of characteristic ODEs, $$ \begin{aligned} \dot{x}(s) & = \nabla_pF(p, z, x) \\ \dot{z}(s) & = \langle p, \dot{x}(s) \rangle = \langle \nabla_p F(p, z, x), p\rangle\\ \dot{p}(s) & = -F_z(p, z, x) \cdot p + \nabla_x F(p, z, x) \end{aligned} $$ And we also have a conservation property, $$ F(p(s), z(s), x(s)) = 0$$ along the flow. Given the boundary condition $\nabla u(x) = G(x), u(x) = f(x),\ x \in \delta \Omega$, one can then simulate the characteristic ODEs using a time stepping scheme by initializing at $x_0 \in \delta \Omega$ and setting $$ x(s_0) \coloneqq x_0 \qquad p(s_0) = G(x_0) \qquad z(s_0) = g(x_0). $$

Solutions

In some cases it is possible to exactly solve the characteristic ODE, or eliminate some variables.

Linear, homogeneous PDE: when $F(p, z, x) = b(x) \cdot p + c(x) z$. Then we can eliminate $p$ from the characteristic system: $$ \begin{aligned} \dot{x}(s) & = b(x) \\ \dot{z}(s) & = \langle p, \dot{x}(s) \rangle = F(p, z, x)-c(x)z \\ & = -c(x)z \end{aligned} $$ which is often easy to solve explicitly by first integrating $\dot{x}(s)= b(x)$, then $\dot{z}(s) = -c(s) z$. This of course recovers the exponential semigroup associated with the linear differential system.

Quasilinear, homogenous PDE: as we will see, quasilinear PDEs also enable the elimination of $p(s)$, leading to a method of solution which is similar to the linear case. A quasilinear PDE corresponds to $F(p, z, s) = b(x, z) \cdot p + c(x, z) z$. Then we have, $$ \begin{aligned} \dot{x}(s) & = b(x, z) \\ \dot{z}(s) & = - c(x, z) z \end{aligned} $$ as above.
