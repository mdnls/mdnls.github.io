Wiener Measure

The Wiener measure is a probability measure over $C(\R_+, \R)$, the space of continuous functions from $\R_+$ to $\R$, endowed with the Borel $\sigma$-algebra corresponding to the topology of uniform convergence on compact sets. This measure coincides with the law of Brownian motion, which is typically constructed as a random variable taking values in $C(\R_+, \R)$.

Definition. the Wiener measure is uniquely identified by the following property. Let $A \subseteq C(\R_+ , \R)$ be a cylinder set, so that: $$ A = \{w \in C(\R_+, \R) : w(t_0) \in A_0,\ w(t_1) \in A_1,\ w(t_n) \in A_n\} $$ for some $0 = t_0 < t_1 < \ldots < t_n$ and some $A_0, \ldots A_n \in \Bcl(\R)$. Then the measure of this set is: $$ \begin{aligned} W(A) & = P(B_{t_0} \in A_0\, B_{t_1} \in A_1, \ldots, B_{t_n} \in A_n) \\ & = 1_{A_0}(0) \int _{A_1 \times \dots \times A_n} \frac{dx_1 \, \dots \, dx_n}{(2 \pi)^{n/2}\sqrt{t_1(t_2-t_1)(\dots)(t_n-t_{n-1})}} \exp\left( - \sum_{i=1}^n \frac{(x_i - x_{i-1})^2}{2(t_i - t_{i-1})}\right) \end{aligned} $$

The fact that this property uniquely identifies the Weiner measure is implied by a monotone class argument. It is not clear a-priori that such a measure exists. However, it can be constructed explicitly as the law of Brownian motion.

Canonical Construction of Brownian Motion

Brownian Motion is typically viewed as a collection of random variables $(B_t(\omega))_{t > 0}$, where $\omega \in \Omega$ is an outcome of some underlying probability space. In contrast, the Wiener measure directly furnishes a probability measure over sample paths: $X \sim W$ is an element of $C(\R_+, \R)$, not a random variable. It is possible to convert $X \sim W$ into a collection of random variables, thereby unifying these two views, and this construction is called the canonical construction of Brownian Motion.

Definition (canonical construction). let $\Wcl= (\Omega, \Fcl, W)$ be the Wiener space, for which $\Omega = C(\R_+, \R)$, $\Fcl$ is the Borel $\sigma$-algebra associated with the compact-open topology, and $W$ is the Wiener measure. For $\omega \in \Omega$, define $B_t(\omega) = \omega(t)$, then the collection $(B_t)_{t > 0}$ is a Brownian motion.

  1. Each $B_t$ is a measurable random variable on $\Wcl$ (convince yourself that the evaluation map $\omega \mapsto \omega(t)$ is measurable for each $t \in \R_+$).
  2. $t \mapsto B_t(\omega)$ is continuous for almost every $\omega$, by construction.
  3. By definition of the Wiener measure, $B_t$ has independent Gaussian increments: for $t > s$, $B_t - B_s \sim \Ncl(0, t-s)$ and it is independent of $\sigma((B_r)_{0 < r < s})$.
