Box-Muller Sampling

The Box-Muller algorithm is a method for sampling Gaussian random variables which improves over Inverse Transform Sampling. It uses the following fact:

Prop. The 2-DOF $\chi^2(2)$ distribution is equal to $\text{Exp}(\lambda = 1/2)$. Proof: let $X_1, X_2 \sim \Ncl(0, 1)$. We can compute the CDF of $X_1^2 + X_2^2$ by integrating in polar coordinates: $$ \begin{aligned} \frac{1}{2\pi} \int_{\R^2} 1[X_1^2 + X_2^2 \leq u]\, e^{-\frac{1}{2}(X_1^2 + X_2^2)}\, dX_1 dX_2 & = \frac{1}{2\pi} \int_0^{2\pi} \int_0 ^\infty 1[r^2 \leq u]\, e^{-\frac{1}{2}r^2} r\, dr\,d\theta \\ & = \frac{1}{2}\int_0^\infty 1[s \leq u]\ e^{-s/2}\, ds. \end{aligned} $$

Algorithm. the idea is to generate a 2-dimensional Gaussian random variable in its polar coordinates.

  1. Sample $R^2 = X_1^2 + X_2^2 \sim \chi^2(2)$. Specifically, apply the Inverse Transform Sampling method to $\text{Exp}(\lambda = 1/2)$.
  2. Sample $\theta \sim \text{Unif}[0, 2\pi]$.
  3. Return $(X_1, X_2) = (R \cos(\theta), R \sin(\theta))$.

Benefits of Box-Muller Sampling

This algorithm addresses a problem with applying the Inverse Transform Sampling method to distributions with light tails. The CDF of such a distribution tends to have extremely flat limits, corresponding to a density which decays quickly. The inverse CDF therefore has extremely steep limits and computing the inverse has a lot of numerical error.

Instead, applying inverse transform sampling to $\text{Exp}(\lambda = 1/2)$ has two valuable benefits!

  1. Exponential tails decay more slowly than Gaussian tails, leading to reduced numerical error.
  2. (!) The inverse Exponential CDF can be computed in closed form and evaluated analytically. The only numerical error to evaluate is floating point error!
