Central Limit Theorem

The central limit theorem is a fundamental type of result in probability that describes the convergence of sums of random variables to an appropriately scaled limiting distribution. The Gaussian CLT, which is an especially popular form, gives conditions on r.v.s $X_1, \ldots, X_n$ under which $$ \frac{1}{\sqrt{n}} \sum_{i=1}^n X_i \stackrel{d}{\longrightarrow} \Ncl(\E[X_0], \Var[X_0]). $$ Some related results include:

  • Poisson Limit Theorem
  • Stein's Method
  • Modes of probabilistic convergence

Central Limit Theorems

Here is an overview of some different useful Gaussian central limit theorems.

Proposition (Lindeberg-LÃ©vy). let $X_1, \ldots, X_n$ be an i.i.d. sequence with mean zero and variance $1$. The sample mean converges in distribution to a Gaussian: $\sqrt{n}\bar{X}_n \stackrel{d}{\to} \Ncl(0, 1)$.

Proof idea: evaluate the log MGF of $\bar{X}_n$, which converges to $\phi(t) = \sigma^2 t^2/2$ as $n \to \infty$.

It is possible to remove the assumption that the sequence is identically distributed, in exchange for a certain growth condition on the $2+\delta$-moments of the sequence.

Proposition (Lyupanov Central Limit Theorem). let $X_1, \ldots, X_n$ be an independent sequence for which $$ \E[X_i] = \mu_i \qquad \Var(X_i) = \sigma_i^2. $$ Suppose further that the sequence satisfies the Lyupanov condition, $$ \lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{1}{s_n^{2 + \delta}} \sum_{i=1}^n \E[|X_i - \mu_i|^{2 + \delta}] $$ where $s_n = \sum_{i \leq n} \sigma_i^2$. Then, $\frac{1}{s_n}\sum_{i=1}^n (X_i - \mu_i) \stackrel{d}{\longrightarrow} \Ncl(0, 1)$.
