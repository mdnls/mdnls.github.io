Cramer-Rao Lower Bound

See also: variational definition of chi-squared divergence.

Theorem: Univariate Cramer-Rao Bound. let $y \mapsto T(y)$ be a sufficient statistic for the family $\{p_\theta : \theta \in \Theta\}$ with expectation $\E_\theta[T(y)] = \Psi(\theta)$. Then $\Var(T(y)) \geq \frac{\Psi'(\theta)^2}{I(\theta)}$.

Proof. Denote by $s_\theta(y)$ the score of $p_\theta(y)$. By Cauchy-Schwartz, $$ \begin{aligned} \Cov_\theta[s_\theta, T(y)]^2 \leq \underbrace{\Var_\theta(s_\theta)}_{I(\theta)} \ \Var_\theta(T(y)) \end{aligned} $$ and the bound follows from the fact that, since $\E_\theta[s_\theta] = 0$, $$ \begin{aligned} \Cov_\theta[s_\theta, T(y)] & = \int \left( \frac{\nabla_\theta p_\theta(y)}{p_\theta(y)} \right)T(y) p_\theta(y) \, dy \\ & = \nabla_\theta \int T(y) p_\theta(y) \, dy \\ & = \Psi'(\theta). \end{aligned} $$

Theorem: Multivariate Cramer-Rao Bound. consider the same setting as above where $T(y) \in \R^m$, $\theta \in \R^d$, and $Y \in \Ycl$. Then, $$ \Cov_\theta(T(y)) \succcurlyeq [D \Psi(\theta)]^T I(\theta)^{-1} [D \Psi(\theta)]. $$

Proof. by the Schur Complement Formula the above inequality holds if and only if the following matrix is positive semidefinite: $$ \Sigma \coloneqq \begin{bmatrix} \Cov_\theta(T(y)) & D \Psi(\theta) \\ [D \Phi(\theta)]^T & I(\theta)\end{bmatrix}. $$ To prove this we can construct a random variable whose covariance is $\Sigma$. Since $I(\theta) = \Cov_\theta(\nabla_\theta \log p_\theta(Y))$, the obvious choice is $$ Z = \begin{bmatrix} T(y) & \nabla_\theta \log p_\theta(Y)\end{bmatrix}. $$ Clearly, $\Cov(Z)$ matches $\Sigma$ on the diagonal blocks. To verify the off diagonal, note that $$ \begin{aligned} \Cov_\theta(T(y), \nabla_\theta \log p_\theta(Y))_{ij} & = \E[(T(Y) - \Psi(\theta))_i \ \partial_{\theta_j} \log p_\theta(Y)] \\ & = \partial_{\theta_j} \int T(y)_i \, p_\theta(y) \, dy \\ & = D\Psi(\theta). \end{aligned} $$ so the claim follows.

Examples/Context

  • Unbiased Estimator: it is common to state the Cramer-Rao lower bound for cases when $\Psi(\theta) = \theta$, so that $T(y)$ is an unbiased estimate of $\theta$.
  • Bounding the MSE: consider the bias $b(\theta) = \Psi(\theta) - \theta$. Then, the MSE can be lower bound,

$$\text{MSE}(T(y)) \geq b(\theta)^2 + \frac{(1+b'(\theta))^2)}{I(\theta)}$$
