Brownian Motion

A Brownian motion is a collection of random variables $(B_t)_{t > 0}$ that defines a continuous function of time, with some special properties. For example, $t \mapsto B_t$ is not differentiable, and the 'roughness' of the path is described by random fluctuations $B_t - B_s \sim \Ncl(0, t-s)$.

Definition (Brownian Motion). let $(\Omega, \Fcl, P)$ be a generic probability space. For $\omega \in \Omega$, a Brownian motion $(B_t(\omega))_{t > 0}$ must satisfy the properties:

  1. (Continuity) $P$-almost surely, $t \mapsto B_t(\omega)$ is continuous and $B_0 = 0$.
  2. (Simple Markov property) For any $t > s$, we have $B_t - B_s \sim \Ncl(0, t-s)$ and this random variable is independent of $\sigma(B_r, r \leq s)$.

It is not necessarily clear whether such a collection of random variables can exist. It turns out that we can construct them explicitly!

Construction of Brownian Motion

There are multiple ways to provide an explicit construction of Brownian motion. One approach is based on modifying an appropriate Gaussian White Noise so that its sample paths are forced to be continuous. Essentially, the strategy is to argue that a stochastic process which satisfies property (2) of the definition must already be 'almost continuous,' in that it behaves like a ($1/2-$ Hölder) continuous function on dense, countable subsets of intervals $I \subseteq \R_+$. Off these dense subsets, we can just modify the process to take values coinciding with the continuous extension of its 'almost continuous' surrogate.

Let $\bar{B}_t$ be Gaussian White Noise on $\R_+$ with intensity $\mu = \mathsf{Leb}$. For simplicity, we will work with the interval $I = [0, 1]$, but the same argument applies to any interval. Let $D^k = \{i 2^{-k} : 0 \leq i \leq 2^k\}$ be the $k$-th dyadic grid on $I$, whose points will be called $t^k_i = i 2^{-k}$.

Proposition (almost continuity). for $P$-almost every $\omega \in \Omega$, and for any $\alpha \in (0, 1/2)$, $$ K_\alpha(\omega) \coloneqq \sup_{k \geq 1} \sup_{i \leq 2^k} \frac{|\bar{B}_{t_{i}^k}(\omega) - \bar{B}_{t_{i+1}^k}(\omega)|}{|t_i^k - t_{i+1}^k|^\alpha} $$ is finite.

Proof. by Markov's inequality, for any $q > 0$, $$ \begin{aligned} P[|\bar{B}_{t_i^k} - \bar{B}_{t^k_{i+1}}| \geq 2^{-\alpha k}] & \leq 2^{q\alpha k}\ \E[|\bar{B}_{t^k_i} - \bar{B}_{t^k_{i+1}}|^q] \\ & \leq 2^{q\alpha k} |t^k_{i} - t^k_{i+1}|^{q/2} \cdot \E_{Z \sim \Ncl(0, 1)} [|Z|^q] \\ & \leq C(q) \ 2^{-kq(1/2 - \alpha)} \end{aligned} $$ For any $\alpha < 1/2$, we may choose sufficiently large $q > (1/2 - \alpha)^{-1}$ such that $q(1/2 - \alpha) = 1 + \eps$ for some $\eps > 0$. Then, by a union bound, $$ P[\cup_{1 \leq i \leq k}\{ |\bar{B}_{t^k_i} - \bar{B}_{t^k_{i+1}}| \geq 2^{-\alpha k}\}] \leq 2^k P[|\bar{B}_{t^k_i} - \bar{B}_{t^k_{i+1}}| \geq 2^{-\alpha k}] \leq 2^{-\eps k} $$ and so, $P$-almost surely, the events $$ A_k = \bigcup_{1 \leq i \leq k} \left\{\left| \bar{B}_i^k - \bar{B}^k_{i+1}\right| \geq 2^{-\alpha k}\right\}\qquad k \geq 0 $$ occur finitely often. But $A_k$ is precisely the event that $$\sup{i \leq 2^k} \frac{|\bar{B}{t{i}^k}(\omega) - \bar{B}{t{i+1}^k}(\omega)|}{|ti^k - t_{i+1}^k|^\alpha} \leq 1 $$ and so, for $P$-almost every $\omega \in \Omega$, $K_\alpha(\omega)$ is a supremum over finitely many numbers.

Remark. this is a simple example of a chaining upper bound! At each scale $k \geq 0$, we proved an upper bound on the fluctuations (w.h.p.) by $2^{-\alpha k}$. It is interesting to contrast this with an argument like that of [[Dudley's Entropy Integral]]. In the present case, we are interested in a relative bound on the fluctuations at each scale, so we prove that $|\bar{B}_{t_{i}^k}(\omega) - \bar{B}_{t_{i+1}^k}(\omega)| / |t_i^k - t_{i+1}^k|^\alpha \lesssim 1$ almost surely. Whereas the point of the entropy integral is to compute an absolute bound on the supremum by summing the bounds across all scales.

**Proposition (unification across scales)** let $D = \cup_{k \geq 0} D^k$. For those $\omega \in \Omega$ such that $$ K\alpha(\omega) \coloneqq \sup{k \geq 1} \sup{i \leq 2^k} \frac{|\bar{B}{t{i}^k}(\omega) - \bar{B}{t{i+1}^k}(\omega)|}{|ti^k - t_{i+1}^k|^\alpha} < \infty, $$ Then it holds for any $s, t \in D$ that $$|\bar{B}_t(\omega) - \bar{B}_s(\omega)| \leq \frac{2 K_\alpha(\omega)}{1-2^{-\alpha}} |t-s|^\alpha$$

Proof sketch. the basic idea is that, for any $t, s \in D$, we will choose the best possible scale for approximating $[t, s]$ by some interval of the form $[i2^{-k}, (i+1)2^{-k}]$, to which we can apply the bound from the previous proposition.

Let $p$ be the smallest integer so that $2^{-p} < t-s$. Let $k \geq 0$ be the smallest integer with $s \leq k2^{-p}$. Then: $$ \begin{align*} s & = k2^{-p} - \eps1 2^{-p-1} - \ldots - \epsl2 ^{-p-l} \\ t & = k 2^{-p} + \eps0' 2^{-p} - \eps1' 2^{-p-1} - \ldots - \eps_l' 2^{-p-l'} \end{align*} $$ for some integers $l'$. The picture is: ![[Screenshot 2024-01-08 at 4.09.49 PM.png]] Set $s_j = k 2^{-p} \sum_{r \leq j} \eps_r 2^{-p-r}$ and define $t_j$ likewise. By the triangle inequality, $$ \begin{align*} |\bar{B}t(\omega) - \bar{B}s(\omega)| & \leq |\bar{B}{t0}(\omega) - \bar{B}{s0}(\omega)| + \sum{j=1}^l |\bar{B}{tj} - \bar{B}{t{j-1}}| + \sum{j=1}^{l'}|\bar{B}{sj} - \bar{B}{s{j-1}}| \\ & \leq K\alpha(\omega) \left(2^{-p\alpha} + 2 \sum{j=1}^\infty 2^{-\alpha(p+j)}\right)\\ & \leq \frac{2 K_\alpha(\omega)}{1-2^{-\alpha}} |t-s|^\alpha. \end{align*} $$ and the claim follows.

We are now finally in a position to construct brownian motion.

**Theorem (Construction of Brownian Motion)** let $\bar{B}_t$ be a Gaussian white noise, as above. For each $\omega \in \Omega$, let $B_t(\omega) = \lim_{s \in D, s \to t} \bar{B}_t$. By the previous propositions, $t \mapsto B_t(\omega)$ is $\alpha$-Hölder continuous, for any $\alpha \in (0, 1/2)$. We claim that $B_t$ is also a [[Modification (Stochastic Process)|modification]] of $\bar{B}_t$ and that any continuous modification of $\bar{B}_t$ is [[Indistinguishable (Stochastic Process)|indistinguishable]] from $B_t$.

Proof. for fixed $t \in \R_+$, the fact that $\bar{B}_s - \bar{B}_t \sim \Ncl(0, s-t)$ implies that $\bar{B}_s \to \bar{B}_t$ in probability as $s \to t$. But then we can take a sequence $s_n \to t$ contained in $D$, for which it is also true $\bar{B}_{s_n} \to B_t$ as $n \to \infty$ by construction. This implies $B_t = \bar{B}_t$ almost surely.

If $Y_t$ is also a continuous modification of $\bar{B}_t$, then consider the event $A = \{B_t = \bar{B}_t = Y_t : t \in D\}$. This event is a countable union of events of the form $\{B_t = \bar{B}_t : t \in \R_+\}$, $\{Y_t = \bar{B}_t : t \in \R_+\}$, which are both almost sure events (by definition of modification). Therefore $P(A) = 1$, implying almost surely that $Y_t = B_t$ for all $t \in \R_+$ (since they are continuous functions that agree on a dense set).

Strong Markov Property

**Proposition**: let $(B_t)_{t \geq 0}$ be Brownian motion and let $T$ be a stopping time that is almost surely finite. Then the random variables $B^{(T)}_t = B_{T + t} - B_T$, $t \geq 0$ form a Brownian motion independent of $\Fcl_T$.

Proof. the idea is to decouple $T$, $B^{(T)}_t$ by rounding the values of $T$. For any continuous bounded function $F(x_1, \ldots, x_n)$ and any $A \in \Fcl_T$, we claim $$ \E[F(B^{(T)}{t1}, \ldots, B^{(T)}{tn})\ 1A] = P(A) \E[F(B{t1}\, \ldots\, B{t_n})] $$ Let $[T]_k = \inf \{k 2^{-n} : T \leq k 2^{-n}\}$. Then (by dominated convergence) $$ \lim{k \to \infty} F(B^{([T]k)}{t1},\ldots,B^{([T]k)}{tn}) = F(B^{(T)}{t1} , \ldots, B^{(T)}{t_n}) $$ so that $$ \begin{align*} \E[F(B^{(T)}{t1}, \ldots, B^{(T)}{tn})\ 1A] & = \sum{i \geq 0}\E\left[ \underbrace{1\{(i-1)2^{-n} < T \leq i2^{-n}\} \ 1A}{(1)}\ \underbrace{F(B^{(i2^{-k})}{t1},\ldots, B^{(i2^{-k})}{tn})}_{(2)}\right]. \end{align*}
