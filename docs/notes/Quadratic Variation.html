Quadratic Variation

Theorem (quadratic variation). let $(X_t)_{t \geq 0}$ be a continuous local martingale. There exists a unique (up to indistinguishability) increasing process $(\langle X\rangle_t)_{t \geq 0}$ such that $X_t^2 - \langle X \rangle_t$ is a continuous local martingale. Furthermore, if $0 = t_0^n < t_1^n < \ldots < t^{n}_{p_n} = t$ is a subdivision of $[0, t]$ with mesh size tending to $0$ as $n \to \infty$, then $$ \langle X\rangle_t = \lim_{n \to \infty} \sum_{i=1}^{p_n} |X_{t_i^n}-X_{t_{i-1}^n}|^2 $$ in probability.

Note. the bracket is a finite variation process since it is monotonically increasing.

An important example of the quadratic variation of a process is that of Brownian motion $(B_t)_{t \geq 0}$, for which $\langle B\rangle_t = t$ has a simple, deterministic form. As another example, if $\langle X \rangle = 0$, then it must be that $X_t = 0$. One way to prove this is to observe that since $X_t^2$ is a continuous local martingale, it is also a supermartingale (apply Fatou to a sequence of localizations $X_{t}^{T_n}$), hence $\E[X_t^2] \leq \E[X_0^2] = 0$ which forces $X_t = 0$ always.

The quadratic variation is also closely connected to the $\Lcl^2$-norm of a continuous local martingale.

Theorem (quadratic variation and L2 boundedness). let $M$ be a continuous local martingale for which $M_0 \in \Lcl^2$. Then the following are equivalent:

  1. $M$ is a true martingale and it is uniformly $\Lcl^2$ bounded.
  2. $\E[\langle M\rangle_\infty] < \infty$.

If these properties hold then $M_t^2 - \langle M \rangle_t$ is a u.i. martingale and $\E[M_\infty^2] = \E[M_0^2] + \E[\langle M\rangle_\infty]$.

Similarly properties hold if $M$ is not uniformly $\Lcl^2$ bounded, in which case the following are equivalent:

  1. $M$ is a true martingale with $M_t \in \Lcl^2$ for each $t \geq 0$.
  2. $\E[\langle M \rangle_t] < \infty$ for every $t \geq 0$.

If these properties hold then $M_t^2 - \langle M \rangle_t$ is a martingale.

Bracket of two Continuous Local Martingales

The quadratic variation $\langle X\rangle_t$, which is itself a quadratic form, can be used to define a bilinear form that takes two stochastic processes and computes a sort of 'inner product' between them.

Definition (bracket of two martingales). let $X, Y$ be continuous local martingales, and define their bracket by, $$ \langle X, Y \rangle_t = \frac{1}{2}\langle X+Y \rangle_t - \langle X \rangle_t - \langle Y \rangle_t $$ The bracket satisfies:

  1. It is the unique finite variation process such that $X_t Y_t - \langle X, Y \rangle_t$ is a continuous local martingale.
  2. The mapping $(X, Y) \mapsto \langle X, Y \rangle$ is bilinear and symmetric.
  3. For an increasing sequence of subdivisions $0 = t_0^n < t_1^n < \ldots < t^n_{p_n} = t$, with mesh tending to zero as $n \to \infty$, we have

$$ \lim_{n \to \infty} \sum_{i=1}^{p_n} (X_{t_i^n}-X_{t_{i-1}^n})(Y_{t_i^n} - Y_{t_{i+1}^n}) = \langle X, Y \rangle_t $$

  1. If $X, Y$ are continuous martingales bounded in $\Lcl^2$ then $X_t Y_t - \langle X, Y \rangle_t$ is a u.i. martingale, $\langle X, Y \rangle_\infty$ is well defined as the a.s. limit of $\langle X, Y \rangle_t$ as $t \to \infty$, it is integrable, and $\E[X_\infty Y_\infty] = \E[X_0 Y_0] + \E[\langle X, Y \rangle_t]$.

Following the notion that $\langle X, Y \rangle$ behaves like an inner product, we can define a notion of orthogonality for random processes.

Definition (orthogonal processes). If $X, Y$ are continuous local martingales and $\langle X, Y \rangle_t =0$ then the processes are said to be orthogonal.

Example. two independent Brownian motions $B_t, B'_t$ must be orthogonal. One way to prove this is to observe that $B_t'' \coloneqq \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(B_t + B_t')$ is a Brownian motion, hence $\langle B''_t \rangle = t = \frac{1}{2}(2 t + 2 \langle B, B'\rangle_t)$ which forces $\langle B, B' \rangle_t = 0$.

It is also true that the bracket satisfies a Cauchy-Schwartz type inequality.

Theorem (Kunita-Watanabe). Let $X, Y$ be two continuous local martingales and let $H, K$ be two measurable processes. Then it is true almost surely that $$ \int_0^\infty |H_s| |K_s| |d \langle X, Y \rangle_s| \leq \left( \int_0^\infty H_s^2 \, d \langle X\rangle_s \right)^{1/2} \left(\int_0^\infty K_s^2 \, \langle Y \rangle_s\right)^{1/2}. $$

Proof sketch. apply Cauchy Schwartz to an increasing partition of $[0, t]$ then take the limit $t \to \infty$.
