Continuous Local Martingales

Definition (continuous local martingale). an adapted process $(X_t)_{t \geq 0}$ with continuous sample paths and with $X_0 = 0$ is a continuous local martingale if there exists an sequence $(T_n)_{n \geq 1}$ of stopping times, such that $T_n \to \infty$, for which the stopped process $X^{T_n}$ is a uniformly integrable martingale for each $n \geq 1$.

In the above definition, we say that the sequence $(T_n)_{n \geq 1}$ localizes the $(X_t)_{t \geq 0}$. Also, note that in the above definition, it is sufficient to request $X^{T_n}$ be merely a martingale,

Continuous Local Martingales and (true) Martingales

Any continuous martingale can be localized by the sequence $T_n = n$ and is therefore a continuous local martingale (to see this, note that the stopped processes $X_{t \land n}$ are all closed by $X_n$ and therefore u.i.).

Proposition (relations between mgs and cts loc mgs).

  1. A nonnegative continuous local martingale $M$ such that $M_0 \in \Lcl^1$ is a supermartingale.
  2. A continuous local martingale $M$ that is dominated by $Z \in \Lcl^1$ (in the sense $|M_t| \leq Z$ for every $t \geq 0$) is a uniformly integrable martingale.
  3. A continuous local martingale $M$ with $M_0 = 0$ is localized by the sequence of stopping times $T_n = \inf \{ t \geq 0 : |M_t| \geq n\}$.

Proof.

  1. Assume WLOG that $M_0 = 0$ (we can subtract out $M_0 \in \Lcl^1$ and treat it separately). Then, for each $n \geq 1$, $M_{t \land T_n} = \E[M_{s \land T_n} \mid \Fcl_t]$, and using Fatou's lemma to take the limit yields $M_t = \lim \inf_{n \to \infty} M_{t \land T_n} \geq \E[M_s \mid \Fcl_t]$.
  2. Repeat the argument in (1) using DCT instead of Fatou.
  3. In this case, the stopped process $M_{t \land T_n}$ is are uniformly dominated by $n$, so by property (2), it is a uniformly integrable martingale. This is precisely the definition of localization!

Continuous Local Martingales and Finite-variation Processes

As motivation for the definition of a semi-martingale, it turns out that we can prove a sort of 'orthogonality' between the space of local martingales and the space of finite variation processes.

Proposition (finite variation continuous local martingales are trivial). Suppose that $(X_t)_{t \geq 0}$ is both a finite variation process and a continuous local martingale. Then $X_t = 0$ for all $t \geq 0$ almost surely.

Proof. the general idea is that if a process has finite variation and finite quadratic variation, then it must be zero. First, let $\tau_k = \inf \{t \geq 0 : \int_0^t |d M_s| \geq k \}$. Since $t \mapsto \int_0^t |d M_s|$ is increasing, we have $M_t^{\tau_n} \leq k$ for all $t \geq 0$.

But, since $\tau_k$ localizes $(M_t)_{t \geq 0}$, we have that $M^{\tau_k}_t$ is a (true) martingale and we can make use of the orthgonality of its increments. For any partition $0 = t_0^n < t_1^n < \ldots < t^n_{p_n} = t$, with mesh size decreasing as $n \to \infty$, we have: $$ \begin{aligned} \E[(M_t^{\tau_k})^2] & = \sum_{i=1}^{p_n} \E[|M_{t_i^n}^{\tau_k} - M_{t_{i-1}^n}^{\tau_k}|^2] \\ & \leq \E\left[\left(\sup_{i=1,\ldots, t_{p_n}}|M_{t_i^n}^{\tau_k} - M_{t_{i-1}^n}^{\tau_k}| \right) \sum_{i=1}^{p_n}|M_{t_i}^{\tau_k}-M_{t_{i-1}}^{\tau_k}|\right] \\ & \leq n \E\left[\sup_{i=1,\ldots,t_{p_n}} |M_{t^n_i}^{\tau_k}-M_{t^n_{i-1}}^{\tau_k}|\right] \end{aligned} $$ Finally, because $M_{t}^{\tau_k}$ is bounded, we may invoke bounded convergence to take $$ \lim_{n \to \infty} \E\left[ \sup_{i=1,\ldots,t_{p_n}} |M_{t_i^n}^{\tau_k} - M^{\tau_k}_{t_{i-1}^n}\right] = 0 $$ which forces $\E[(M^{\tau_k}_t)^2] = 0$. It follows that $M_t = 0$ for all $t \geq 0$.
